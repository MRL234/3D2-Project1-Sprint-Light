Terra Lumina

Keith Sonnier


❍ Keith Sonnier is a Postminimalist video and light artist. Sonnier created his first artwork using neon in 1968.

❍ A neon tube is a sealed glass tube with a metal electrode at each end, filled with one of a number of gases at low pressure.

❍ He has been one of the most successful with this technique.

❍ His pieces tend to include light tubes, aluminum and other objects to create electric light scuptrures.

https://www.alconlighting.com/blog/home/led-innovations-art-top-artists-use-light-medium/

The Great Wave


❍ The Great Wave of Kanawaga, also known as The Great Wave, is one of the most famous examples of Japanese art in the world.

❍ The most eye-catching features of the painting is the extended wave as it is about to break with the crash of its claw-like crest.

❍ The beautiful dark blue pigment used by Hokusai, clled Prussian Blue, was a new material at the time.

❍ The wave is about to strike the boats as if it were an enormous monter, one which seems to symbolise the irresistible force of nature and the weakness of human beings.

❍ This blue color and the wave gave me some idea of creating my light.

https://theculturetrip.com/asia/japan/articles/hokusai-s-great-wave/

Koinobori


❍ Carp-shaped windsocks traditionally flown in Japan to celebrate Tango no sekku, a traditional calendrical event which is now designated a national holiday: Children's day

❍ Landscapes across Japan are decorated with Koinobori from April to early May, in honor of children for a good future and in the hope that they will row up healthy and strong

Kinetic Sculpture


❍ Kinetic Sculpture relies on movement for visual and emotional impact.

❍ It has taken a wide variety of forms, from motorised mechanics to naturally powered elements in both indoor and outdoor settings.

❍ Artist Theo Jansen writes "Kinetic art was created by artists who pushed the boundaries of traditional,static art forms to introduce visual experiences that would engage the audience and profoundly change the course of modern art."
